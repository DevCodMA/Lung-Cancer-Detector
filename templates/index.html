LUNG CANCER DETECTOR

Bringing Clarity to Your Health!

Lung cancer is a malignant tumor that originates in the cells of the lungs, which are vital organs responsible for oxygen exchange during breathing. There are different types of lung cancer, with the most common being non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), which includes three main subtypes: Adenocarcinoma, Large cell carcinoma, and Squamous cell carcinoma. Each subtype has distinct characteristics and behaviors.

Adenocarcinoma is the most common subtype of NSCLC and is often found in the outer part of the lungs. It tends to grow slowly and is associated with a history of smoking, as well as other risk factors such as exposure to environmental toxins. Adenocarcinoma is known to develop from glandular cells, and it can spread to other parts of the body if not detected and treated early.

Large cell carcinoma, also known as undifferentiated carcinoma, is a less common subtype of NSCLC. It is characterized by large, abnormal-looking cells and tends to grow rapidly. Large cell carcinoma can occur in any part of the lungs and is often aggressive, with a higher risk of metastasis.

Squamous cell carcinoma, also known as epidermoid carcinoma, typically arises in the bronchial tubes, which are the air passages leading to the lungs. It is often associated with a history of smoking and is known to develop from squamous cells that line the airways. Squamous cell carcinoma tends to grow slowly, but it can spread to nearby lymph nodes and other organs.

ABOUT PROJECT

The "Advancing Lung Cancer Detection: A Multi-Class Approach with Xception Architecture and Transfer Learning Techniques" project is a cutting-edge research endeavor that aims to enhance lung cancer detection using advanced deep learning techniques. The project utilized a dataset of 1000 CT scans, which were carefully curated to include diverse cases representing different stages and types of lung cancer and it achieved an impressive accuracy rate of 77% in detecting different types of lung cancer from CT scans.

The project leveraged the Xception architecture, a state-of-the-art convolutional neural network (CNN) model known for its exceptional performance in image classification tasks. The Xception architecture is characterized by its depth and complexity, allowing it to capture intricate features from medical images with high accuracy. The deep layers of the Xception model enable it to learn and represent complex patterns and features from the CT scans, making it well-suited for the task of lung cancer detection.

Additionally, transfer learning techniques were employed in the project to further enhance the model's performance. Transfer learning allows the model to leverage pre-trained weights from another task or dataset, which helps in speeding up the training process and improving the model's generalization ability. By leveraging transfer learning, the model was able to learn from a larger and more diverse dataset, resulting in improved accuracy and robustness.

The multi-class approach adopted in the project allows for the detection of different types of lung cancer, including adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and small cell carcinoma, among others. This approach enables the model to classify the lung cancer cases into different categories with high precision, facilitating early detection and accurate diagnosis.
